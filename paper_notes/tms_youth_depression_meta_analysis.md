# Efficacy and safety of transcranial magnetic stimulation in the treatment of children, adolescents and young adults with depression: A meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials

## Basic info

* System / intervention: Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS), with subgroup analyses for HF-rTMS, LF-rTMS, bilateral TMS, and iTBS
* Authors: Xiaoman Wang, Zhuoran Zhang, Yanlin Li, Ying Gao, Qingjiu Cao
* Year: 2026
* Venue / source: Journal of Affective Disorders, volume 392, article 120132
* DOI: 10.1016/j.jad.2025.120132
* Link: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jad.2025.120132
* Date read: 2026-03-26
* Why selected in one sentence: Youth depression treatment options are weak, TMS is getting pushed harder, and the useful question is whether the evidence in children/adolescents/young adults is actually solid or just enthusiastic aggregation.

## Quick verdict

**Promising but overstated. The pooled result is large, but the evidence base looks very heterogeneous and likely too messy to support clean confidence.**

This paper is useful as a map of the current youth-TMS literature, not as definitive proof that TMS is settled for depressed young people. The headline effect is very large, and HF-rTMS appears to drive most of the benefit, but the abstract already shows a major warning sign: **I² = 93%**. That is not “minor between-study noise.” That is “these studies differ a lot, so the pooled number is unstable and should not be treated like a single clean truth.”

## One-paragraph overview

This is a meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials testing active TMS versus sham or no stimulation in children, adolescents, and young adults with depression. The authors searched multiple English- and Chinese-language databases through February 2025 and included **34 RCTs with 2705 total participants**. Their primary outcome was change in depression severity, with safety and adverse events as secondary outcomes. The main pooled finding is that TMS significantly reduced depression severity overall (**SMD = -1.55, 95% CI -1.88 to -1.23, p < 0.001**), with **high-frequency rTMS (HF-rTMS)** showing the strongest subgroup effect (**SMD = -1.90**). Low-frequency and bilateral protocols showed smaller benefits, while **iTBS did not show a significant effect**. The paper concludes that TMS is effective and generally safe in this population, especially HF-rTMS.

## Key questions this summary must address

### 1. What problem is the paper trying to solve?
Youth depression is common, serious, and badly served by current treatment options. Medication efficacy is limited, side effects matter, and treatment resistance is real. TMS works well enough in adults to be clinically important, so the paper asks whether that benefit extends to younger populations.

### 2. What exactly was analyzed?
The paper pooled **randomized controlled trials** comparing active TMS against **sham or no stimulation** in depressed children, adolescents, and young adults. It is not a broad catch-all of uncontrolled clinical reports. That is good. But the population and intervention bucket is still broad: different ages, different diagnostic criteria, different control conditions, and different TMS protocols.

### 3. What is the main efficacy result?
The pooled antidepressant effect is large:
- **SMD = -1.55**
- **95% CI: -1.88 to -1.23**
- **p < 0.001**

On paper, that is a strong result.

But the more important sentence is the one attached to it:
- **I² = 93%**

That degree of heterogeneity means the studies are not behaving like clean replications of the same underlying effect. So the pooled estimate may point in a useful direction, but it should not be treated as a precise answer.

### 4. Which TMS protocol looked best?
According to the reported subgroup analysis:
- **HF-rTMS** had the largest apparent benefit (**SMD = -1.90, 95% CI -2.42 to -1.37**) 
- **LF-rTMS** and **bilateral TMS** showed smaller effects
- **iTBS** was **not significant**

So if you take the paper at face value, the practical implication is not “all TMS works.” It is closer to “the evidence currently favors HF-rTMS, while the iTBS story in youth is not ready.”

### 5. What safety claim does the paper make?
The authors say TMS was generally safe and report:
- **OR = 1.713, 95% CI 1.422 to 2.064, p < 0.001**

This deserves caution.

That number, as written, is directionally odd if interpreted as odds of adverse events, because an OR above 1 usually means the event is **more common** in the treatment group. So the safety claim likely depends on how the outcome was coded, or on the distinction between mostly mild/transient adverse events versus serious harms. Based on the abstract alone, the safety interpretation is not cleanly explained.

In other words: **the paper may well be right that TMS is tolerable, but the abstract’s safety statistic is not self-explanatory and should not be repeated lazily.**

### 6. What are the biggest strengths?
- It restricts to **RCTs**, which is the right starting point.
- It covers a clinically important population where evidence is still thin.
- It includes **34 studies / 2705 participants**, which is larger than many older youth-TMS summaries.
- It separates protocol classes instead of pretending all TMS is one thing.
- It tests moderation by diagnostic criteria and control condition.

### 7. What are the biggest weaknesses or red flags?
- **Very high heterogeneity (I² = 93%)**. This is the main problem.
- The age bucket is broad: **children, adolescents, and young adults** are not a single clinical population.
- “TMS” bundles together several protocols with different mechanistic and practical profiles.
- Control groups differ (**sham vs no stimulation**), which can inflate or distort pooled estimates.
- The abstract signals that efficacy varies by **diagnostic criteria** and **control condition**, which means results are sensitive to study design choices.
- A meta-analysis can produce a big clean-looking number while sitting on top of a noisy literature.

### 8. What does this paper actually let us say?
A defensible reading is:
- the youth-TMS literature is now large enough that a pooled antidepressant signal is hard to dismiss outright
- **HF-rTMS is the most credible protocol in this meta-analysis**
- **iTBS is not yet supported here**
- the evidence base is still too heterogeneous to justify overconfident “TMS clearly works for youth depression” messaging

So the paper supports **promise**, not closure.

### 9. What would I want to inspect in the full paper before trusting the headline effect?
- risk-of-bias distribution across included RCTs
- how much of the dataset comes from small single-center studies
- whether publication bias / small-study effects were formally tested and what happened
- whether sham-controlled trials alone still show a robust effect
- whether adolescent-only analyses differ from mixed youth/young-adult analyses
- how adverse events were defined and why the reported OR still supports the “safe” framing
- whether medication co-treatment was balanced or inconsistent across studies

### 10. Why does this matter for cabbageland?
Because this is exactly the kind of paper where a huge pooled effect can seduce people into premature certainty.

The right move is to separate:
- **signal exists**
- from
- **signal is stable, clinically generalizable, and protocol-resolved**

This paper helps with the first claim more than the second.

## My actual read

This is not junk. It is a useful paper. But it is also the sort of meta-analysis that can make a field look more settled than it really is.

A pooled **SMD of -1.55** is enormous by clinical-trial standards. That immediately raises two possibilities:
1. TMS really is very effective in this population, or
2. the pooled literature contains enough heterogeneity, design looseness, and small-study inflation to exaggerate the true effect.

Given the **I² = 93%**, I would not bet on the simple heroic interpretation.

The protocol breakdown is the most actionable part. If I had to keep one practical conclusion, it would be:
- **HF-rTMS has the strongest case**
- **iTBS does not yet**

The safety framing also needs discipline. “Generally safe” may be true in the clinically important sense of no obvious major catastrophic signal, but the abstract statistic is not clean enough to quote without explanation.

So the paper is useful, but mainly as **evidence of momentum plus unresolved variance**, not as a final verdict.

## Final decision

**Keep, but annotate with skepticism. Cite for “promising evidence with HF-rTMS strongest; literature still highly heterogeneous,” not for “case closed.”**

Best framing:
- TMS in youth depression looks increasingly plausible
- HF-rTMS is the most supported protocol here
- the magnitude of benefit is probably less certain than the headline pooled effect suggests
- safety/tolerability appears acceptable, but the abstract-level statistic needs closer reading before confident repetition
